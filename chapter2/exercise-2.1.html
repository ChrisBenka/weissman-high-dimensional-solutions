Claim: There exists a non-negative random variable \(X\) and \(a > 0\) such that \[ \mathbb{P}(X \geq a) = \frac{\mathbb{E}[X]}{a}, \] i.e., Markov's inequality holds with equality.
Proof:
Consider a random variable \(X\) defined as \[ X = \begin{cases} a, & \text{with probability } p, \\ 0, & \text{with probability } 1 - p, \end{cases} \] where \(a > 0\) and \(0 \leq p \leq 1\).
1. Compute the expectation: \[ \mathbb{E}[X] = a \cdot p + 0 \cdot (1-p) = a p. \]
2. Compute the probability \(\mathbb{P}(X \geq a)\): \[ \mathbb{P}(X \geq a) = \mathbb{P}(X = a) = p. \]
3. Substitute the expectation into Markovâ€™s inequality: \[ \mathbb{P}(X \geq a) \leq \frac{\mathbb{E}[X]}{a} = \frac{a p}{a} = p. \]
Since \(\mathbb{P}(X \geq a) = p\), we have \[ \mathbb{P}(X \geq a) = \frac{\mathbb{E}[X]}{a}. \] Hence, Markov's inequality holds with equality for this choice of \(X\).
